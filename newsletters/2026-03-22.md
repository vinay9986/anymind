# Weekly AI Research & Policy Brief – Late March 2026

*National standards, brittle reasoning, and concentrated power at the AI

## Executive Summary

The White House’s new National AI Legislative Framework is the clearest
statement so far of how the Trump Administration wants to govern artificial
intelligence: through a single, national standard set in Washington, with
“targeted preemption” of state AI laws.[E1][E2] Released March 20, 2026, it
lays out seven guiding priorities for Congress: protecting children and
empowering parents; safeguarding and strengthening American communities;
respecting IP and supporting creators; preventing censorship and protecting
free speech; enabling innovation and U.S. AI dominance; educating an
AI‑ready workforce; and establishing a federal framework that overrides
conflicting state rules.[E1][E2][E3]

The Framework is a legislative blueprint, not binding law: it creates no
immediate obligations and directs no specific regulatory actions.[E2] It
builds on Executive Order 14365, which ordered a federal review of “onerous”
state AI laws and set up an AI Litigation Task Force,[E2][E3] and it sits
alongside a much more detailed, 291‑page TRUMP AMERICA AI Act draft that
would codify these ideas and further constrain state‑level
experimentation.[E2]

## Lead Story – The National AI Legislative Framework

### How We Got Here

Executive Order 14365, “Removing Barriers to American Leadership in
Artificial Intelligence,” signed in December 2025, initiated a coordinated
review of state‑level AI laws, directed agencies to develop national policy
recommendations, and required the Commerce Secretary to identify “onerous”
state measures.[E2][E3] It also paved the way for an AI Litigation Task
Force tasked with challenging state laws viewed as unconstitutional or
preempted.[E2]

The new Framework is the public, political synthesis of that work: it tells
Congress what kind of national AI regime the White House wants and why state
experimentation should be narrowed.[E1][E2]

### The Seven Pillars of the Framework

The Framework organizes its legislative recommendations across seven
themes:[E1][E2][E3]

1. **Protecting children and empowering parents** – calls on Congress to
give parents stronger tools such as account controls and privacy
protections, and to require AI platforms likely accessed by minors to
implement features that reduce risks like sexual exploitation or
encouragement of self‑harm.[E1]
2. **Safeguarding and strengthening American communities** – links AI
development to economic growth, small‑business resilience, and energy
“dominance.”[E1]
3. **Respecting IP and supporting creators** – argues that American creative
works and identities must be respected even as AI systems need to make fair
use of what they “learn from the world.”[E1]
4. **Preventing censorship and protecting free speech** – insists AI must
not become a vehicle for suppressing lawful political expression or
enforcing “right‑ and wrong‑think.”[E1]
5. **Enabling innovation and ensuring American AI dominance** – calls on
Congress to remove outdated or unnecessary barriers to innovation,
accelerate AI deployment, and broaden access to testing environments.[E1]
6. **Educating an AI‑ready workforce** – emphasizes workforce development
and skills training so American workers can participate in AI‑driven
growth.[E1]
7. **Establishing a federal framework with targeted preemption** – warns
that a “patchwork of conflicting state laws” will undercut innovation and
U.S. leadership, arguing for a uniform national regime.[E1][E2][E3]

### From Blueprint to Law

Analyses emphasize that the Framework is not binding and does not, on its
own, create new legal obligations or direct agencies to undertake specific
regulatory actions.[E2]

By contrast, the TRUMP AMERICA AI Act discussion draft released March 18,
2026, is a 291‑page bill that seeks to codify elements of existing
AI‑related executive orders, impose detailed requirements on AI developers,
and constrain states’ ability to regulate AI systems.[E2]

## Trends & Market Dynamics

### Capability Hype vs. Reasoning Limits

New frontier models—GPT‑5.4, Claude Opus 4.6, and Gemini 3.1 Pro—were all
reported in March 2026 as major upgrades, including million‑token‑class
context for GPT‑5.4, stronger coding and reasoning benchmarks for Opus 4.6,
and competitive multimodal reasoning and lower pricing for Gemini 3.1
Pro.[E2] The ARC‑AGI‑3 benchmark, highlighted in independent coverage, found
all frontier models scoring under 1% on reasoning puzzles that humans solve
at roughly 100% accuracy.[E2]

### Capital Concentrates at the Frontier

TechCrunch and venture data show AI startups taking a large share of venture
dollars, with about 10% of companies capturing half the funding, including
mega‑rounds for OpenAI, Anthropic, and xAI.[E4] February 2026 saw a record
$189 billion in global startup funding, driven heavily by OpenAI’s $110B,
Anthropic’s $30B, and Waymo’s $16B rounds.[E4][E4a]

### Agentic AI Security: Big Spend, Uneven Coverage

Top agentic AI security startups have raised about $3.6 billion in aggregate
funding, while broader cybersecurity M&A has crossed roughly $96
billion.[E4] In contrast, total disclosed funding for MCP‑focused security
startups (Operant AI, Runlayer, Helmet Security, Manufact) is about $40
million for a protocol with more than 17,000 deployed servers.[E4]

### Neurodata, Fairness, and Legal Alignment

Meta open‑sourced TRIBE v2, a foundation model trained to predict human
brain activity in response to visual, auditory, and linguistic stimuli,
using data from approximately 700 volunteers.[E2]

Fairness and explainability work this month included new papers on “Learning
Optimal Individualized Decision Rules with Conditional Demographic Parity”
and on “the asymmetric Shapley value as an honest importance metric for
high‑dimensional features,” both listed in the March 2026 stat.ML arXiv.[E3]

## What This Means for Operators (In Brief)

- Design governance and controls assuming eventual national standards and
targeted preemption, but continue to track and comply with state AI rules
until Congress and courts clarify preemption.[E1][E2][E3]
- Treat GPT‑5.x, Claude, Gemini, and Qwen primarily as powerful assistants,
not autonomous decision‑makers, in light of ARC‑AGI‑3’s sub‑1% scores on
human‑trivial reasoning tasks.[E2]
- Expect capital and capabilities to keep concentrating around a few
frontier labs and infra providers, as shown by mega‑rounds for OpenAI,
Anthropic, xAI, and infra raises like Nscale and Nebius.[E4][E4a][E5]
- Stand up a dedicated AI security program that includes model, data, agent,
and protocol layers, paying particular attention to MCP servers, which
currently have only ~$40M in dedicated security funding despite broad
deployment.[E4]
- Begin integrating fairness and explainability tooling grounded in methods
like conditional demographic parity and asymmetric Shapley values, which are
now appearing in the March 2026 ML literature.[E3]
- Treat neuromodel and biometric work—exemplified by TRIBE v2—as a special
high‑sensitivity category requiring elevated consent, governance, and
privacy controls.[E2]
